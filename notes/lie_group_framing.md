## The question

Is $\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3$ a Lie group?

**Strict answer: no.** A Lie group is a smooth manifold; the lattice is discrete. The lattice's spatial structure is a *discrete subgroup* of $\mathbb{R}^3$, not a Lie group.

**Useful answer: the lattice is built from, and acted upon by, Lie groups at every level**, and the framework's central open problem is fundamentally a Lie-theoretic question. The right way to think about $\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3$ is as a **crystallographic representation** of a continuous Lie-group structure, in the same sense that an ordinary crystal is a discrete representation of the Euclidean group.

So the framework lives in a Lie-theoretic landscape. The lattice is the discrete part; the dynamics, the spinor structure, and the conjectured symmetries are all continuous Lie groups.

## The crystallographic-representation framing

The mathematical structure of the framework is:

$$
\boxed{\;\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3 \;=\; \bigl(\text{discrete subgroup } \Gamma \text{ of } SE(3)\bigr)\;\otimes\;\bigl(SU(2)\text{-spinor at each node, normalized by }\mathcal{A}=1\bigr)\;}
$$

This is structurally identical to how an ordinary crystal carries a unitary representation of its space group, with two extras:

- **Spinor structure:** each node carries an $SU(2)$ representation rather than a scalar.
- **$\mathcal{A}=1$ constraint:** each node's spinor amplitude lives on the unit 3-sphere of its $SU(2)$ representation, with normalization preserved by every tick.

The dynamics (tick rule) is then a **unitary representation of $\Gamma \times U(1)$** acting on the $SU(2)$-spinor field over $\Gamma$. Each tick is one element of that representation: spatial translation by a basis vector, combined with a spinor rotation by $\omega$.

This connects directly to lattice gauge theory (Kogut-Susskind, Wilson). Lattice QCD is the same kind of object: discrete spatial subgroup carrying a continuous internal symmetry representation, with a discrete tick (Wilson loop) carrying the dynamics. The framework's bipartite RGB/CMY structure is the analogue of staggered fermions; the $\mathcal{A}=1$ constraint is the unitarity contract that ordinary lattice gauge theory imposes through the link variables.

## How today's findings sit in this structure

Three immediate connections:

### 1. The optical axis is a commutative direction in the lattice's Lie algebra

The discrete subgroup $\Gamma$ of $SE(3)$ has Lie-algebra-valued generators along the basis vectors $\pm\mathbf{V}_i$. These don't commute in general — the bipartite RGB/CMY structure breaks commutativity in most directions. **The unique direction along which all three RGB generators commute is $\mathbf{V}_1 + \mathbf{V}_2 + \mathbf{V}_3 = (1,1,-1)$.**

This is the same direction that comes out of the SymPy calculation as the eigenvalue-zero direction of $M_\text{eff}$ — the optical axis of the lattice's birefringence. **The geometric finding (flat dispersion direction) and the Lie-theoretic finding (commutative subalgebra direction) are the same thing.** That's not coincidence; it's the same fact viewed from two angles.

This also identifies *why* the holographic depth lives along $(1,1,-1)$: the depth direction is the commutative subalgebra, and propagation along it doesn't engage the non-abelian structure of the lattice. It's the abelian direction in a non-abelian crystal.

### 2. The fractal mass spectrum is a Cantor subset of the Lie group $U(1)$

Time evolution is parametrized by the rotation rate $\omega \in [0, 2\pi]$, which is the compact Lie group $U(1)$. The KAM theorem applied to the symplectic tick map says that stable orbits exist only at certain values of $\omega$, forming a **Cantor subset of $U(1)$**.

Each stable particle in the framework corresponds to one "good" point in this Cantor subset. The Standard Model mass spectrum is the **catalog of dimension-resonant points** on $U(1)$ where the lattice's bipartite structure admits stable closed orbits.

This rephrases §14's "fractal universe" claim in Lie-group language: the universe's discrete particle content is a Cantor subset of the Lie group $U(1)$ that parametrizes lattice time evolution. Not "the universe is a fractal," but **"the universe's particle content is the fractal subset of a Lie group at which the bipartite tick rule produces stable orbits."**

### 3. The central conjecture IS a Lie-group calculation

The abstract states that the gauge structure of the Standard Model is "the minimal set of session types required to maintain $\mathcal{A}=1$." The conjecture in the conclusion makes this precise:

$$
\mathrm{Aut}(\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3, \mathcal{A}=1) \stackrel{?}{=} SO(3,1) \times SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)
$$

This is a **specific Lie-group calculation** that either succeeds or fails. The framework either derives the Standard Model or it doesn't, depending on whether this equation is true.

The calculation has the form: take the lattice $\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3$ as a discrete subgroup-with-decoration; find all symmetries (linear maps on the discrete set) that preserve the bipartite structure, the basis vectors, and the $\mathcal{A}=1$ constraint; check whether the resulting symmetry group is $SO(3,1) \times SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$.

This is not aspirational; it's a finite-dimensional Lie-algebra calculation that should be tractable. The answer either matches the Standard Model or doesn't. This is the framework's most important falsifiability handle.

## Why this matters

The framework's most ambitious claim — that the Standard Model is forced by $\mathcal{A}=1$ on a bipartite octahedral lattice — has been stated programmatically in the paper but never precisely. The Lie-theoretic framing makes it a **specific finite-dimensional calculation**. Either $\mathrm{Aut}(\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3, \mathcal{A}=1) = SO(3,1) \times SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$ or it doesn't. The answer is a finite computation that current symbolic-algebra tools can in principle perform.

Phrased this way, the framework is no longer "an ambitious program with a few results." It becomes "a specific Lie-theoretic conjecture with a tractable verification path." That is a fundamentally different kind of paper.

If the calculation succeeds: the Standard Model is derived from $\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3 + \mathcal{A}=1$.

If it fails: the framework is constrained to its current verified scope (QM, gravity, Born rule, hydrogen) and the SM-derivation claim is dropped.

Either outcome is a paper.
