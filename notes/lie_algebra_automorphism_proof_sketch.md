*Working notes, 2026-05-05.  Drafts a Tier 2 v1.0 proof item for the
central Lie-theoretic conjecture identified in
`notes/lie_group_framing.md` (2026-04-27):*

$$
\mathrm{Aut}(\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3, \mathcal{A}=1)
\;\stackrel{?}{=}\;
SO(3,1) \,\times\, SU(3) \,\times\, SU(2) \,\times\, U(1).
$$

*Targets `paper/sections/conclusion.tex` (the §15 automorphism
conjecture) and `paper/sections/induced_gauge_action.tex` (gauge
sector) for revision.  This is a proof skeleton + strategy at the
same depth as `notes/born_rule_gleason_uniqueness.md` and

---

## What the conjecture says

Three structures define $\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3 + \mathcal{A}=1$:

1. The discrete spatial subgroup $\Gamma \subset SE(3)$ generated by
   the basis vectors $\{\pm\mathbf{V}_i : i=1,2,3\}$.
2. The bipartite parity $\mathbb{Z}_2$ partition into RGB and CMY
   sublattices, with $\mathbf{V}^\text{CMY}_i = -\mathbf{V}^\text{RGB}_i$.
3. The per-site complex amplitude space $\mathbb{C}^2 = \mathbb{C}\{\psi_R, \psi_L\}$
   carrying the $\mathcal{A}=1$ normalisation.

An *automorphism* is a linear map on the resulting space that preserves
all three structures simultaneously.  The conjecture identifies the
group of such automorphisms with $SO(3,1) \times SU(3) \times SU(2)
\times U(1)$ — the Standard Model gauge group plus Lorentz.

This is *not* a fitting claim.  The dimension count of the conjectured
automorphism group is

$$
\dim[SO(3,1) \times SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)]
\;=\; 6 + 8 + 3 + 1 \;=\; 18.
$$

The lattice's continuum automorphism group either has 18 dimensions
matching this product structure, or it has a different dimension and
the conjecture is refuted.  The answer is a finite calculation.

## The structure of the proof

The argument has five steps:

1. **Discrete automorphism group.**  Compute the finite group of
   linear maps preserving $(\Gamma, \mathbb{Z}_2, \{\pm\mathbf{V}_i\})$.
   This is a finite enumeration; expected to give $O_h \rtimes \mathbb{Z}_2$.
2. **Continuum lift via $O_h$-averaging.**  Lift to the continuum limit;
   discrete $O_h$ averaging produces the $SO(3,1)$ continuous Lorentz
   (already done, §6 of the paper).
3. **Internal U(1).**  Per-site $\psi \mapsto e^{i\theta}\psi$ phase
   rotation commutes with the tick rule and preserves $\mathcal{A}=1$.
   This is a $U(1)$ acting trivially on the spatial structure but
   nontrivially on the per-site amplitude.  Generator counted: $+1$.
4. **Internal SU(2).**  Per-site $(\psi_R, \psi_L) \mapsto U(\psi_R, \psi_L)$
   for $U \in SU(2)$ commutes with the bipartite parity (the parity
   maps $R \leftrightarrow L$, which is one specific element of $SU(2)$).
   This is an $SU(2)$ acting on each site's spinor.  Generator
   count: $+3$.
5. **Internal SU(3).**  The novel piece.  The three RGB basis vectors
   define a triplet structure; promoted to a complex internal triplet
   (each basis vector carrying its own complex amplitude in a
   3-dimensional internal space), the natural symmetry is $SU(3)$
   acting on the colour labels.  Generator count: $+8$.

The five steps must combine to give the full $18$-dimensional
automorphism Lie algebra.  Steps 1, 2, 3, 4 are *already proven* (or
partially) in existing paper sections.  Step 5 is the central open
calculation; it carries the full structural weight of the SM-derivation
claim.

## Step 1 — discrete automorphism group of $(\Gamma, \mathbb{Z}_2, \{\pm\mathbf{V}_i\})$

The discrete spatial subgroup $\Gamma$ is generated by the six basis
vectors $\pm\mathbf{V}_1 = \pm(1,1,1), \pm\mathbf{V}_2 = \pm(1,-1,-1),
\pm\mathbf{V}_3 = \pm(-1,1,-1)$.  An automorphism of $\Gamma$ is a linear
map $A : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ that permutes the six basis
vectors and preserves the RGB/CMY parity (must send RGB triplet to
RGB triplet, possibly via the parity flip).

The group of such $A$ is a subgroup of $O(3)$ (real orthogonal $3\times 3$
matrices).  It must:

- preserve the *unsigned* set $\{\mathbf{V}_1, \mathbf{V}_2, \mathbf{V}_3,
  -\mathbf{V}_1, -\mathbf{V}_2, -\mathbf{V}_3\}$ (the basis-vector set),
- act consistently with the bipartite parity (RGB → RGB or RGB → CMY).

The first condition gives the cubic point group $O_h$ (order 48).  The
bipartite parity adds a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ that either preserves or swaps
RGB and CMY; the swap is implemented by the linear map $A = -I$ (multiply
by $-1$), which is itself an element of $O_h$.

So the discrete automorphism group is

$$
\mathrm{Aut}_\text{discrete}(\Gamma, \mathbb{Z}_2, \{\pm\mathbf{V}_i\})
\;=\; O_h.
$$

This is a finite group of order 48 with no continuous part.  In the
continuum limit, it lifts to $SO(3)$ (the connected component) plus
parity ($\mathbb{Z}_2$).  This is the claim from
`notes/lie_group_framing.md`.

**Expected dimension count after lifting**: 3 (rotations) + 3 (boosts
emerging from O_h-averaging in §6) = 6 = $\dim SO(3,1)$.

The boosts are not in $O_h$ directly; they emerge as the *Lorentzian
counterpart* of the rotational $O_h$ structure when the Dirac equation's
$O_h$-averaging is extended to spacetime.  This is the §6 derivation
already in the paper; the Lie-theoretic interpretation is that the
continuum lift of $\mathrm{Aut}_\text{discrete}$ generates the full
$SO(3,1)$.

## Step 2 — continuum lift gives $SO(3,1)$

Section 6 of the paper (`paper/sections/emergent_kinematics.tex`) shows
that the bipartite tick rule's continuum limit produces a Lorentz-
invariant Dirac equation through $O_h$-averaging of the directional
gradient terms.  This is the *spacetime* part of the automorphism Lie
algebra; the connected component is $SO(3,1)$.

The Lie-theoretic translation:

$$
\mathrm{Aut}_\text{spacetime}(\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3 \to \text{continuum})
\;=\; SO(3,1).
$$

This is *6 dimensions* of the conjectured 18.  Already done, modulo
the formal Lie-theoretic statement of what §6 derives — which is the
content of `notes/lie_group_framing.md`'s §1.

## Step 3 — internal $U(1)$ from per-session phase

Per-site phase rotation $\psi \mapsto e^{i\theta}\psi$ at every node
preserves:

- $|\psi|^2$ (and hence $\mathcal{A}=1$).
- The bipartite parity (RGB and CMY transform identically).
- The basis-vector set (no spatial action).
- The hop probabilities $p_\text{move}, p_\text{stay}$ (these depend on
  $|\psi|^2$, not the phase).

So $U(1)$ is an automorphism of the structure.  This is $+1$ dimension.

The induced action on $\mathcal{H}_\text{session}$ is the standard
$U(1)$ phase-rotation generator $\hat{Q}$, which is the global charge
operator.  In standard QM language: this is the conserved quantity
canonically conjugate to $\theta$ (Noether's theorem applied to the
$U(1)$-invariance).

The framework's $\mathcal{A}=1$ axiom *is* the $U(1)$-charge conservation
law for the per-session amplitude.  This identification is the central
insight of `notes/conservation_of_probability.md`: the $U(1)$ here is
the same as the electromagnetic $U(1)_\text{em}$ when extended across
the lattice via the gauge connection (`paper/sections/induced_gauge_action.tex`).

## Step 4 — internal $SU(2)$ from per-session bipartite chirality

The per-site spinor $(\psi_R, \psi_L) \in \mathbb{C}^2$ admits
$SU(2)$ rotations $U \in SU(2)$:

$$
\begin{pmatrix}\psi_R \\ \psi_L\end{pmatrix}
\;\mapsto\;
U \begin{pmatrix}\psi_R \\ \psi_L\end{pmatrix},
\qquad U \in SU(2).
$$

For this to be an automorphism of $(\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3, \mathcal{A}=1)$:

- $\mathcal{A}=1$: $U$ unitary $\Rightarrow$ $|\psi_R|^2 + |\psi_L|^2$
  preserved.  ✓
- Bipartite parity: the parity map sends $\psi_R \leftrightarrow \psi_L$,
  which is one specific element of $SU(2)$ (Pauli $\sigma_x$).  $U$
  must commute with $\sigma_x$ for the parity to be respected, OR $U$
  must transform the parity automorphism by conjugation in a way that
  preserves the parity action's order.  The full $SU(2)$ does the
  latter: any $U \in SU(2)$ sends the parity automorphism to a
  conjugate element of $SU(2)$, which is again a valid parity map.

So the full $SU(2)$ acts as automorphism.  This is $+3$ dimensions.

The induced action on $\mathcal{H}_\text{session}$ is the standard
$SU(2)$ doublet rotation generator $\hat{T}^a$ ($a = 1, 2, 3$), which
is the weak isospin operator.  The bipartite parity is exactly the
chirality basis of weak interactions: $\psi_R$ and $\psi_L$ as right-
and left-chiral components.

The Standard Model identification is therefore $SU(2) \cong SU(2)_W$
(weak isospin).  The bipartite parity is the chirality structure that
distinguishes weak-isospin doublets from singlets.

## Step 5 — internal $SU(3)$ from RGB triplet (the open piece)

This is the central open calculation.  The framework has *three* RGB
basis vectors $\mathbf{V}_1, \mathbf{V}_2, \mathbf{V}_3$ at every site.
At the lattice level, these are real vectors and the natural symmetry
is the *real* permutation group on three indices ($S_3$, order 6).

For $SU(3)$ to emerge, the three basis vectors must be promoted to a
**complex internal triplet**: each basis vector $\mathbf{V}_i$ carries
its own per-site complex amplitude $\chi_i \in \mathbb{C}$, with the
internal triplet $(\chi_1, \chi_2, \chi_3) \in \mathbb{C}^3$ subject to
$|\chi_1|^2 + |\chi_2|^2 + |\chi_3|^2 = 1$ (a triplet $\mathcal{A}=1$).

**The proposed mechanism.**  The kinetic step at each tick has the form

$$
\psi(\mathbf{x}, t+1) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{3} \alpha_i\, \mathrm{Hop}_{\mathbf{V}_i}(\psi(\mathbf{x}, t)),
$$

where $\alpha_i$ are weights satisfying $\sum_i |\alpha_i|^2 = 1$.  In
the current paper, $\alpha_i = 1/\sqrt{3}$ uniformly.  But the
$\mathcal{A}=1$ axiom does not *force* uniformity at the level of the
kinetic step; it only forces the global probability balance.  The
three weights $\alpha_i$ form a complex triplet with $|\alpha|^2 = 1$,
and a $SU(3)$ rotation among them sends one valid kinetic-step weight
choice to another.

The conjecture: $SU(3)$ acts on the basis-vector triplet weights
$(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3)$ at each site.  Different choices of
weights produce *different but physically equivalent* kinetic steps;
the symmetry is the gauge symmetry that identifies them.

**What needs to be verified to close this:**

(a) The bipartite parity maps the RGB triplet $(\alpha_1, \alpha_2,
   \alpha_3)$ to the CMY triplet via $\alpha^\text{CMY}_i = (\alpha_i)^*$
   or similar.  The parity is one specific element of $SU(3)$.

(b) Different $SU(3)$ weight choices produce dynamically equivalent
   evolutions modulo gauge transformations on the connection field.
   This is the gauge-theory equivalence.

(c) The frame condition $\sum_\text{RGB} \mathbf{V}_i \mathbf{V}_i^T = 3 I$
   must be invariant under $SU(3)$ rotation of the weights.  Since the
   frame condition is real and symmetric under permutation of indices,
   only the modulus structure $|\alpha_i|$ matters; the phases $\arg
   \alpha_i$ are free to rotate by $SU(3)$ without disturbing the
   frame.  This is plausible but needs explicit verification.

(d) The three-loop structure of $SU(3)$ commutators (the eight
   Gell-Mann generators, structure constants $f^{abc}$) must be
   recoverable from the explicit basis-vector geometry.  This is the
   detailed Lie-algebra match.

If all four hold, then $SU(3)$ acts as automorphism and contributes
$+8$ dimensions, completing the conjectured 18.

**Where the structural difficulty is.**  Standard QCD has $SU(3)$
acting on a fundamental triplet of *quark colour states*.  The
framework's basis-vector triplet is *spatial* (three real geometric
directions), not internal.  The promotion of spatial-triplet to
internal-complex-triplet weights is the key conceptual step.  Two
candidate mechanisms:

- **Per-tick weight gauge.**  As sketched above: the weights
  $\alpha_i$ are free per site, $SU(3)$-rotatable.  The gauge field
  that relates different weight choices on adjacent sites is the
  gluon field.  This is closest to lattice gauge theory's standard
  formulation.

- **Spinor-decoration of basis vectors.**  Each basis vector carries
  its own RGB/CMY-paired pair of spinor amplitudes; the three pairs
  form a colour triplet.  $SU(3)$ acts on the colour labels.  This
  requires the spinor structure to factorise per basis vector, which
  is more elaborate but cleaner geometrically.

A definitive Lie-algebra calculation is needed to distinguish these,
or to show they are equivalent (different gauges of the same group).

## Why the *direct product* structure?

The conjecture says $SO(3,1) \times SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$,
not a more general semidirect or twisted product.  This is a strong
claim.

The natural reason: the four factors act on **independent structural
features** of the lattice:

- $SO(3,1)$: spatial-temporal coordinates.
- $SU(3)$: weights of the three basis vectors (colour).
- $SU(2)$: per-site bipartite chirality (weak).
- $U(1)$: per-site overall phase (electromagnetic).

If these features are genuinely independent (no entanglement between
the four), the symmetry product is direct.  If they entangle (e.g.,
spatial rotations interact non-trivially with colour rotations), the
product is semidirect or twisted.

Standard Model phenomenology says the product *is* direct, except for
electroweak unification ($SU(2) \times U(1) \to SU(2) \times U(1)$ is
direct at the gauge-group level; the Higgs breaks it to $U(1)_\text{em}$
non-directly).  So the conjecture matches phenomenology.

The lattice-level statement: the four structural features of
$\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3$ — spatial, weight, chirality, phase — are
mutually orthogonal in the Lie-algebra sense.  Verifying this
orthogonality is part of Step 5's calculation: the $SU(3)$ generators
must commute with the $SU(2)$, $U(1)$, and Lorentz generators.

## Strategy for the explicit calculation

Following the action-items list from `notes/lie_group_framing.md`:

### (a) Compute $\mathrm{Aut}(\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3, \mathcal{A}=1)$ explicitly.

Using GAP or SymPy with a Lie-algebra package:

1. Encode the lattice as a discrete graph with bipartite labelling and
   per-site $\mathbb{C}^2$ amplitudes.
2. Enumerate all linear maps $A : \mathbb{R}^3 \otimes \mathbb{C}^2 \to
   \mathbb{R}^3 \otimes \mathbb{C}^2$ that:
   - permute the basis-vector set $\{\pm\mathbf{V}_i\}$,
   - preserve the bipartite parity,
   - preserve the per-site $\mathcal{A}=1$ norm,
   - commute with the kinetic-step weights $\alpha_i$.
3. Take the connected component (continuous Lie subgroup) of the
   resulting group.
4. Compare its Lie algebra to $\mathfrak{so}(3,1) \oplus \mathfrak{su}(3)
   \oplus \mathfrak{su}(2) \oplus \mathfrak{u}(1)$ via dimension count
   and structure constants.

This is an explicit finite-dimensional Lie-algebra computation.  The
expected result is the conjectured product; if a different group falls
out, the conjecture is refuted.

### (b) Identify the gauge connection field.

For each generator of the automorphism Lie algebra, the corresponding
*gauge field* on the lattice is the connection that relates different
gauge choices on adjacent sites.  This connects to
`paper/sections/induced_gauge_action.tex` (the induced gauge action
discussion already drafted in v0.98-RC).

### (c) Verify direct-product structure.

Show that the four blocks of the automorphism Lie algebra commute with
each other in pairs.  If any pair has non-trivial structure constants,
the product is not direct, and the conjecture must be revised
(typically to a semidirect or twisted form).

## Subtleties to address explicitly

### (i) The conjecture is not "the lattice contains the SM gauge group" but "the lattice's automorphism group *is* the SM gauge group plus Lorentz".

Containment is weak: many structures contain the SM gauge group.  The
strong claim is that the automorphism group is *exactly* this product,
no more no less.  An automorphism group with extra factors would
introduce new gauge bosons; an automorphism group missing a factor
would fail to predict the SM.

The strict equality is the falsifiability handle.  If the Lie-algebra
calculation produces, say, $SO(3,1) \times SU(3) \times SU(2) \times
U(1) \times U(1)'$ with an extra $U(1)'$, the framework predicts an
extra gauge boson the Standard Model does not have.  That would be a
*falsifiable prediction*, and depending on phenomenological constraints
on extra $U(1)$'s, it might already be ruled out.  Either outcome is
informative.

### (ii) Spinor-decoration vs weight-gauge for $SU(3)$.

The two candidate mechanisms in Step 5 (per-tick weight gauge vs
spinor-decoration of basis vectors) have not been distinguished here.
The Lie-algebra calculation should select between them or show they
are gauge-equivalent.  If they give different structure constants, the
selection is meaningful; if they give the same constants, the framework
is gauge-invariant under the choice.

### (iii) The Higgs sector.

The conjecture as stated is the **unbroken** gauge group.  Symmetry
breaking $SU(2) \times U(1) \to U(1)_\text{em}$ via Higgs vev is a
*dynamical* phenomenon (vacuum structure), not part of the static
automorphism group.  The framework would need a Higgs-analogue
mechanism to break the symmetry; this is a separate research target
beyond the gauge-group identification.

### (iv) The conjecture and the Lorentz-vs-Galilei distinction.

$SO(3,1)$ is Lorentzian, not Galilean.  The lattice produces $SO(3,1)$
because the bipartite tick rule has a finite "speed limit" $c = a/\tau$
(Step 6 of the Dirac derivation).  A non-relativistic lattice (no
finite speed) would produce Galilean spatial automorphisms instead.
This is the structural reason the framework is automatically
relativistic, not by postulate.

### (v) The 18 dimensions are the *real* Lie-algebra dimensions.

For $SO(3,1)$, the 6 dimensions are real (3 rotations + 3 boosts).  For
$SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$, the 8+3+1 = 12 are also real Lie-
algebra dimensions.  Total: 18.  The lattice's automorphism Lie algebra
is real (the lattice is real and the maps are real-linear), so the
counting is consistent.

## Proposed patch to `paper/sections/conclusion.tex` (§15 conjecture)

The current §15 states the automorphism conjecture programmatically.
The patch replaces it with the precise Lie-algebra statement:

```latex
\subsection{The Lie-algebra automorphism conjecture}
\label{subsec:automorphism_conjecture}

The framework's most ambitious claim is that the gauge structure of
the Standard Model is the automorphism group of $\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3$
combined with $\mathcal{A}=1$:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Aut}(\mathcal{T}_\diamond^3, \mathcal{A}=1)
\;=\; SO(3,1) \times SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1).
\label{eq:automorphism_conjecture}
\end{equation}

This is a finite-dimensional Lie-algebra equality: both sides have
real dimension 18 ($6 + 8 + 3 + 1$).  The match is more than
dimensional --- the structure constants of the lattice's automorphism
algebra must reproduce those of the right-hand side, factor by factor.

The five contributing structures are:

\begin{itemize}
\item $SO(3,1)$: continuum lift of the cubic point group $O_h$ acting
on the spatial sub-lattice, plus boosts emerging from the $O_h$-averaged
Dirac equation (\S\ref{subsec:dirac_limit}).  Dimension 6.
\item $SU(2)$: per-site rotation of the bipartite chiral spinor
$(\psi_R, \psi_L)$, with the bipartite parity identified as $\sigma_x \in
SU(2)$.  Dimension 3.
\item $U(1)$: per-site phase rotation $\psi \mapsto e^{i\theta}\psi$;
the $\mathcal{A}=1$ conservation law is exactly the corresponding
Noether charge.  Dimension 1.
\item $SU(3)$: rotation of the per-site weights $(\alpha_1, \alpha_2,
\alpha_3) \in \mathbb{C}^3$ assigned to the three RGB basis vectors,
with the frame condition $\sum_i \mathbf{V}_i \mathbf{V}_i^T = 3 I$
determining the modulus structure $|\alpha_i|^2 = 1/3$ and leaving the
phases $\arg \alpha_i$ free.  Dimension 8.
\end{itemize}

The identification of $SU(3)$ as the colour gauge group --- of weak
isospin $SU(2)_W$ with the chiral $SU(2)$, of $U(1)_Y$ with the phase
$U(1)$, of Lorentz $SO(3,1)$ with the spatial-temporal $O_h$-averaged
group --- is internal to the lattice structure.  The direct-product
form is conjectured because the four contributing structures are
mutually orthogonal in the Lie-algebra sense (the spatial, weight,
chirality, and phase factors of the lattice are independent at the
infinitesimal level).

A complete proof requires explicit computation of the lattice's
automorphism Lie-algebra structure constants and verification that
they reproduce those of $\mathfrak{so}(3,1) \oplus \mathfrak{su}(3) \oplus
\mathfrak{su}(2) \oplus \mathfrak{u}(1)$.  This is a tractable
finite-dimensional computation using existing Lie-algebra software (GAP,
SymPy~\cite{sympy_lie}).  The verification is the central open problem
of the program; resolution either confirms or refutes the framework's
most ambitious claim.

If Eq.~\ref{eq:automorphism_conjecture} succeeds, the Standard Model
gauge group is derived from a single conservation axiom on a discrete
substrate.  If it fails, the framework's verified scope is the
relativistic Hilbert-space + gravity + Born-rule structures of \S6,
\S7, and \S10, with the SM-derivation claim explicitly retracted.
Either outcome is a complete result; the question is the calculation,
not the framing.
```

Approximate length: ~70 lines of LaTeX, half a page.  Citations: new
key `sympy_lie` for the SymPy Lie-algebra package documentation, plus
existing references to standard QFT.  No figures.

  does not predict three families of fermions.  Whether the lattice's
  automorphism group has a $\mathbb{Z}_3$ outer automorphism that
  generates the family structure is a follow-on conjecture worth
  stating.
- **Gravity-gauge unification.**  $SO(3,1)$ in
  Eq.~\ref{eq:automorphism_conjecture} is the *spacetime* automorphism;
  $SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$ are *internal* automorphisms.  The
  framework treats them as direct-product factors of the same Lie
  algebra; standard physics treats spacetime and internal symmetries as
  fundamentally different.  Whether this unification is a feature
  (gravity is also a gauge theory in the framework) or a bug (the
  conjecture overreaches) is a structural question for follow-up
  papers.
