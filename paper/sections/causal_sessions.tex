\label{sec:causal_sessions}

A particle in the $\unity$ framework is not a point mass, a field mode,
or a wavefunction on a background space.
It is a \emph{causal session}: a persistent probability flux that
carries an internal clock, evolves by the bipartite tick rule, and
obeys the $\unity$ constraint at every tick.
This section defines the session structure and identifies its algebraic
content: the internal clock is a U(1) oscillator whose group element encodes
phase and whose algebra generator encodes mass.

% ── 3.1 The Phase Oscillator: U(1) Group and Algebra ───────────────────────────────
\subsection{The Phase Oscillator: U(1) Group and Algebra}
\label{subsec:phase_oscillator}

Each causal session carries a \emph{phase oscillator} --- an internal oscillator
that advances by a fixed angle $\omega$ per tick.
The oscillator state at tick $n$ is the unit complex number
\begin{equation}
    r_n = e^{i\phi_n} \in \mathrm{U}(1), \qquad \phi_n = n\omega \mod 2\pi.
    \label{eq:oscillator_state}
\end{equation}
Two structures are at work here, and they are distinct.

\textbf{The group element} $r_n = e^{i\phi_n}$ lives on the unit circle
$\mathrm{U}(1) = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : |z| = 1\}$.
This is the Lie group: a one-dimensional compact manifold with group
multiplication given by phase addition.
The $\unity$ constraint --- total probability equal to one at every tick ---
is the requirement that the oscillator never leaves this manifold.
$\mathcal{A} = 1$ is not a normalisation convention; it is the statement
that the session's state is always a group element, never a rescaled one.
Probability is conserved because U(1) is a group, not because we
choose to enforce it.

\textbf{The algebra generator} $\omega \in \mathfrak{u}(1) \cong \mathbb{R}$
is the element of the Lie algebra that exponentiates into the group via
\begin{equation}
    r_{n+1} = e^{i\omega} \cdot r_n.
    \label{eq:oscillator_advance}
\end{equation}
This is left-multiplication by $e^{i\omega}$: a rotation of the oscillator
by angle $\omega$ per tick.
The generator $\omega$ is the particle's \emph{instruction frequency} ---
the rate at which its internal clock advances.
In the continuum limit $a \to 0$ with $\omega/a = m$ held fixed,
$\omega$ becomes the rest mass (Section~\ref{subsec:dirac_limit}).
Mass is the generator of the internal U(1) rotation, not a separately
postulated property.

\paragraph{The phase mismatch as local Hamiltonian.}
When the session is at a node with topological potential $V(\mathbf{x})$,
the effective advance per tick is
\begin{equation}
    \delta\phi(\mathbf{x}) = \omega + V(\mathbf{x}).
    \label{eq:phase_mismatch}
\end{equation}
This is the discrete Hamiltonian evaluated locally: an algebra element
$\omega + V \in \mathfrak{u}(1)$ that varies from node to node.
The potential $V$ deforms the algebra element without leaving the Lie
algebra --- the sum of two real numbers is still a real number, so the
phase mismatch is still a valid generator.
The hop probability
\begin{equation}
    p_\mathrm{stay} = \sin^2\!\tfrac{\delta\phi}{2}, \qquad
    p_\mathrm{hop}  = \cos^2\!\tfrac{\delta\phi}{2},
    \label{eq:hop_probs}
\end{equation}
is the norm-squared of the U(1) rotation by $\delta\phi/2$.
It depends only on the algebra element $\delta\phi$, not on the
current group element $r_n$.
The dynamics are controlled by the algebra; the group element tracks
the accumulated phase.

\paragraph{Why this matters.}
In standard quantum mechanics the wavefunction $\psi$ is a complex
number and the Hamiltonian $H$ is a Hermitian operator.
Their relationship is through the Schr\"odinger equation, which must
be postulated.
Here the relationship is structural: the Hamiltonian is a u(1) algebra
element, the state is a U(1) group element, and the equation of motion
$r_{n+1} = e^{i(\omega+V)} \cdot r_n$ is the definition of
exponentiation from the algebra to the group.
The Schr\"odinger equation is not postulated; it is the definition of
what it means for the oscillator to advance.

% ── 3.2 The CausalSession: Spinor Amplitude on the Lattice ───────────────────
\subsection{The Causal Session: Spinor Amplitude on the Lattice}
\label{subsec:causal_session}

The phase oscillator governs the \emph{internal} clock.
The \emph{external} amplitude --- the probability density distributed
across the lattice --- is the spinor field $\Psi = (\psi_R, \psi_L)^T$,
where $\psi_R(\mathbf{x})$ is the amplitude on the RGB sublattice
and $\psi_L(\mathbf{x})$ on the CMY sublattice.

These are not two independent fields.
They are the same session viewed on even and odd ticks respectively:
$\psi_R$ is the right-handed component (advances on RGB directions,
even ticks) and $\psi_L$ is the left-handed component (advances on
CMY directions, odd ticks).
The bipartite alternation forces the two-component structure.
Spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ is not a separate postulate; it is the consequence
of there being exactly two sublattices.

The $\unity$ constraint on the spinor is
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{\mathbf{x}} \bigl(|\psi_R(\mathbf{x})|^2
                           + |\psi_L(\mathbf{x})|^2\bigr) = 1.
    \label{eq:unity_spinor_session}
\end{equation}
This is the field-theoretic extension of the oscillator constraint
$|r_n| = 1$: the total probability across all nodes and both spinor
components is exactly one at every tick.
The session is a U(1) oscillator distributed over the lattice.

\paragraph{Massless sessions.}
When $\omega = 0$ the phase mismatch $\delta\phi = V(\mathbf{x})$
vanishes on a flat lattice, so $p_\mathrm{stay} = 0$ and
$p_\mathrm{hop} = 1$.
The amplitude hops on every tick, alternating strictly between the
two sublattices, and propagates at $c = 1$ node/tick.
The two-component structure collapses: at any instant the amplitude
is entirely on one sublattice.
This is the photon.
Its generator $\omega = 0$ is the identity element of the algebra;
it has no mass, no ZB oscillation, and no internal clock frequency.

% ── 3.3 The Tick Rule as U(1) Gauge Transformation ───────────────────────────
\subsection{The Tick Rule as Gauge Transformation}
\label{subsec:gauge}

The tick rule advances the oscillator by $e^{i\delta\phi}$ and hops the
spinor amplitude to neighbouring nodes.
Viewed globally, each tick is a \emph{local} U(1) gauge transformation:
different nodes receive different phase advances $\delta\phi(\mathbf{x})$
depending on the local potential $V(\mathbf{x})$.

The $\unity$ constraint is preserved by this transformation because
U(1) is compact: $|e^{i\delta\phi}| = 1$ regardless of $\delta\phi$.
No phase advance, however large, can change the norm of the oscillator.
The constraint is therefore automatically satisfied at every node and
every tick without additional enforcement beyond the tick rule itself.

This structure is the discrete origin of U(1) gauge invariance in
quantum electrodynamics.
The gauge field $A_\mu$ of QED is, in this framework, the potential
$V(\mathbf{x},t)$ that deforms the local algebra element from $\omega$
to $\omega + V$.
The gauge transformation $\psi \to e^{i\alpha(\mathbf{x})}\psi$ is
the freedom to redefine the zero of the phase at each node — a freedom
that exists precisely because the physics depends only on the algebra
element $\delta\phi$, not on the accumulated group element $r_n$.

The implication for the gauge group of the Standard Model is direct:
U(1) electromagnetism is the phase symmetry of a single session.
The larger gauge group SU(3)$\times$SU(2)$\times$U(1) arises when
multiple sessions with different RGB/CMY cone orientations interact —
the subject of Section~\ref{sec:vacuum_twist}.

% ── 3.4 Inertia as Phase-Gradient Stability ───────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Inertia as Phase-Gradient Stability}
\label{subsec:inertia}

A session in uniform motion carries a spatial phase gradient
$\theta$ per lattice edge.
The gradient is an element of the algebra: it is the spatial part of
the local generator $\omega + V + \theta$.
Inertia is the \emph{stability} of this gradient against perturbation.

Perturbing the gradient by $\Delta\theta$ changes the hop probability by
$\Delta p_\mathrm{hop} \propto -\sin(\omega)\,\Delta\theta$.
The larger $\omega$ (the heavier the particle), the smaller the relative
change in hop probability for a given perturbation.
Heavier particles are more resistant to gradient changes — they have
more inertia.

Experiment \texttt{exp\_01} confirms this.
A Gaussian wavepacket ($\omega = 0.2$, $\mathbf{k} = (k,k,k)$) drifts
at $v_x = 0.046$ nodes/tick; a heavier packet ($\omega = 0.5$, same
$\mathbf{k}$) drifts measurably slower.
The velocity formula $v = \tfrac{c}{2}\sin(\omega)\sin(\theta)$
(derived in Section~\ref{subsec:momentum_emergence}) gives the correct
ratio.
Inertia is not postulated; it follows from the algebra element $\omega$
governing the oscillator's response to gradient perturbations.

\paragraph{Summary.}
A causal session is a U(1) oscillator ($e^{i\phi} \in \mathrm{U}(1)$)
distributed over the bipartite lattice as a two-component spinor
$(\psi_R, \psi_L)$.
Its mass is the algebra generator $\omega \in \mathfrak{u}(1)$.
Its dynamics are the exponentiation of the local algebra element
$\delta\phi = \omega + V$ into the group at every tick.
Probability conservation is the compactness of U(1).
Spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ is the bipartite structure.
Inertia is the gradient stability of the generator.
None of these are postulated; they are the content of the single
statement: \emph{a particle is a persistent U(1) flux on a bipartite
lattice}.
